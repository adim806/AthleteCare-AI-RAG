FC Velocity — Return-to-Play Protocol
SOURCE: return_to_play.txt | DOMAIN: clinical_protocols | UPDATED: November 2024
CHUNK_FORMAT: RTP_ENTRY | Each stage and criterion is self-contained and independently retrievable.

CORE PRINCIPLE: Time alone does not determine readiness. A player must meet both functional and psychological criteria before advancing each stage. The final decision to return a player to competition is made jointly by the physiotherapist, Dr. Shapira, and the head coach.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_STAGE
STAGE: 1 | NAME: Rest and protection
GOAL: Protect the injured tissue and prevent secondary damage.

Q: What is RTP Stage 1?
A: Stage 1 is complete rest from all sport activity. General conditioning (upper body, pool) is maintained where possible to preserve fitness.

Q: What are the criteria to progress from RTP Stage 1 to Stage 2?
A: No pain at rest, swelling fully resolved, and full weight-bearing restored for lower limb injuries.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_STAGE
STAGE: 2 | NAME: Light aerobic exercise
GOAL: Restore cardiovascular fitness without loading the injured structure.

Q: What is RTP Stage 2?
A: Light aerobic exercise that avoids loading the injured structure. Permitted activities: walking, pool running, stationary cycling, upper body gym work.

Q: What are the criteria to progress from RTP Stage 2 to Stage 3?
A: Zero pain or swelling during and after all Stage 2 activities. Subjective pain score 0/10 throughout.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_STAGE
STAGE: 3 | NAME: Sport-specific individual exercise
GOAL: Restore football movement patterns without team contact.

Q: What is RTP Stage 3?
A: Sport-specific individual exercise. Permitted activities: straight-line jogging, non-contact ball work, passing drills.

Q: What are the criteria to progress from RTP Stage 3 to Stage 4?
A: No symptoms at Stage 3 load. Injured limb strength at minimum 80% of contralateral limb. Physiotherapist confirmation.

Q: What strength level is required at RTP Stage 3?
A: The injured limb must reach at least 80% of the contralateral limb's strength before progressing to Stage 4.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_STAGE
STAGE: 4 | NAME: Non-contact team training
GOAL: Reintegrate the player into the team environment without collision risk.

Q: What is RTP Stage 4?
A: Full team training sessions excluding physical challenges and collisions. No contact drills.

Q: What are the criteria to progress from RTP Stage 4 to Stage 5?
A: Full Stage 4 sessions completed without symptom recurrence. Injured limb strength at minimum 90% of contralateral limb. Single-leg hop test LSI ≥ 90%. Physician clearance for Grade II injuries and all surgical cases.

Q: What strength level is required at RTP Stage 4?
A: The injured limb must reach at least 90% of the contralateral limb's strength on isokinetic testing.

Q: What is the LSI threshold required at Stage 4?
A: Single-leg hop test Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) ≥ 90%. Calculated as: injured limb distance ÷ uninjured limb distance × 100.

Q: Who must clear a Grade II injury before Stage 4?
A: Both the responsible physiotherapist and Dr. Anat Shapira must provide clearance before a player with a Grade II injury or surgical history advances to Stage 4.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_STAGE
STAGE: 5 | NAME: Full training and competition
GOAL: Full participation in all training and competitive matches.

Q: What is RTP Stage 5?
A: Full participation in all training and competition, including contact and match play.

Q: What are the criteria to complete RTP Stage 5 and return to competitive play?
A: All functional tests passed. Psychological readiness confirmed (ACL-RSI score ≥65 for ACL injuries). Head coach agreement. Final sign-off by Dr. Anat Shapira.

Q: Can a player return to competition without head coach agreement?
A: No. Return to competitive play requires agreement from the physiotherapist, Dr. Shapira, and the head coach.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_FUNCTIONAL_TESTS
RECORD_TYPE: Functional tests for RTP clearance

Q: What is the single-leg hop test and what is the threshold?
A: The player hops forward on one leg. The injured limb must reach at least 90% of the uninjured limb's distance. LSI = injured ÷ uninjured × 100. Threshold: LSI ≥ 90%.

Q: What is the triple hop test threshold?
A: Three consecutive hops on one leg — same 90% LSI threshold as the single-leg hop test.

Q: What are the isokinetic strength criteria for RTP?
A: H:Q ratio must be 0.60–0.70. Absolute strength of the injured limb must be ≥ 90% of the contralateral limb at 60°/s.

Q: What sprint speed is required for RTP clearance?
A: The player must achieve at least 95% of their pre-injury maximum sprint speed on GPS tracking.

Q: What is the agility test standard for RTP?
A: 5-10-5 agility test completion time must be within 10% of the player's pre-injury baseline.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_PSYCHOLOGICAL
SECTION: Psychological readiness

Q: What psychological assessment is used for ACL RTP clearance?
A: The ACL-RSI (ACL Return to Sport after Injury) questionnaire. A score of 65 or above on a 0–100 scale is required before Stage 5 clearance.

Q: What is the ACL-RSI threshold for returning to competition?
A: ≥65 out of 100. A score below 65 means the player is NOT psychologically cleared, regardless of physical readiness.

Q: What is the psychological assessment for non-ACL injuries?
A: A clinical interview by the physiotherapist assessing confidence and willingness to engage in contact. If a player expresses fear of re-injury, graduated exposure to contact during Stage 4 is the appropriate response — the player must not be rushed.

Q: Can a player be cleared physically but not psychologically?
A: Yes. Physical and psychological criteria are independent. A player who passes all functional tests but scores below 65 on ACL-RSI is not cleared for Stage 5. Both must be satisfied.

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_TIMELINES
SECTION: Return timelines by injury type

Q: How long does a Grade I hamstring strain take to return?
A: 5–10 days. This is a minimum — all criteria must be met regardless of elapsed time.

Q: How long does a Grade II hamstring strain take to return?
A: 3–6 weeks. Isokinetic dynamometer testing is mandatory before Stage 4 clearance.

Q: How long does a Grade I calf strain take to return?
A: 5–10 days.

Q: How long does a Grade I groin strain take to return?
A: 7–14 days.

Q: How long does a Grade I ankle sprain take to return?
A: 5–7 days.

Q: How long does a Grade II ankle sprain take to return?
A: 2–4 weeks.

Q: How long does a Grade I knee MCL sprain take to return?
A: 1–2 weeks.

Q: How long does a Grade II knee MCL sprain take to return?
A: 4–8 weeks.

Q: How long does ACL reconstruction rehabilitation take?
A: Return to training: 9–10 months. Return to competitive play: 10–12 months. Both are minimums — criteria must be met.

Q: How long does a tibial or metatarsal stress fracture take to return?
A: Return to training: 10–14 weeks. Return to full competition: 14–18 weeks.

Q: What is the minimum return time after concussion?
A: 5 days minimum, following the 6-step graduated concussion RTP protocol with 24 hours symptom-free between each step.

Q: Does meeting the time threshold mean a player can return?
A: No. All timelines are minimums. A player cannot advance regardless of time elapsed unless all functional and psychological criteria are met.

---

RECORD_TYPE: RTP_REINJURY_REVIEW
SECTION: Re-injury review policy

Q: What triggers a re-injury review at FC Velocity?
A: A re-injury to the same structure within 6 months of returning to play triggers a formal review at the next medical team meeting.

Q: What does the re-injury review examine?
A: Whether the RTP protocol was followed correctly, whether functional criteria were genuinely met, and whether external pressure from coaching staff influenced the clearance decision.

Q: What must be filed after a re-injury review?
A: A re-injury review report must be filed in KineticMind within 7 days of the re-injury.
